# Negotiation Playbook — Trade Launch Intelligence
# Practical Negotiation Frameworks for Traders & Exporters

## Overview
Most first-time traders lose money not in the market but at the
negotiation table. This playbook covers every major negotiation
a trade business encounters — from buying from suppliers to
closing buyers, booking freight, and securing bank financing.

## 1. CORE NEGOTIATION PRINCIPLES

### The 5 Rules That Apply to Every Negotiation

**Rule 1 — Whoever is more desperate loses.**
Never negotiate when you need the deal urgently. Build enough
pipeline (multiple buyers, multiple suppliers) so you can walk away.
One deal, one supplier, one buyer = zero leverage.

**Rule 2 — Silence is a weapon.**
After you state your price or terms, stop talking. The next person
to speak gives ground. Most people fill silence with concessions.
Let the other party fill the silence.

**Rule 3 — Never accept the first offer.**
Even if the first offer is acceptable, push back once. It's expected.
"I appreciate the offer — I was hoping to do better than that. Can
we look at [specific item]?" If they hold firm, you can accept.
If you accept immediately, you'll always wonder if you left money
on the table — and they'll know it.

**Rule 4 — Anchor high when selling, anchor low when buying.**
Your first number sets the psychological range of the negotiation.
If you're selling at $100 and you open at $100, the deal closes at $85.
If you open at $115, the deal closes at $98.

**Rule 5 — Trade concessions, don't give them.**
Every concession you make should get something in return.
"I can move on price if you can confirm the order today / increase
the volume / provide a 30% advance / give me a 12-month contract."
Free concessions train the other party to keep asking.

## 2. SUPPLIER / PROCUREMENT NEGOTIATION

### What You're Negotiating
- Purchase price (per kg, per ton, per unit)
- Payment terms (advance % vs. on delivery)
- Quality guarantee and rejection policy
- Minimum order quantity (MOQ)
- Delivery timeline and consistency
- Price stability (fixed vs. market-linked)

### Before You Negotiate
- Know the current market price (AFEX, Tridge, FAO GIEWS, broker quotes)
- Have 3+ competing supplier quotes — use them as leverage
- Know your maximum acceptable price (your unit economics must work)
- Know the supplier's pain points — do they need cash flow? Quick turnover?

### The Price Conversation Framework

**Opening:**
"I've been looking at a few suppliers for [product]. The range I'm
seeing for [Grade X] is [low end of your research] to [mid-range].
I'd like to work with someone consistent for a long time. What's
your best price for [volume] per month?"

**If price is too high:**
"I appreciate that. The challenge is I'm working to a specific margin
to make this work. Is there any flexibility if I commit to [volume]
per month for [X months]? Or if I pay faster than your standard terms?"

**The volume-for-price trade:**
"If I can guarantee [2× current volume] per month, what does your
price look like at that scale?"

**The commitment trade:**
"If I sign a 6-month supply agreement with fixed volumes, can we
lock in a better price per ton?"

### Payment Terms to Push For
| Term | What to Ask For | Why |
|---|---|---|
| New supplier | Pay 30% advance, 70% on delivery | Reduces your exposure |
| Trusted supplier | 100% on delivery or 7-day credit | Preserves your cash flow |
| Large orders | 20% advance, 80% against quality inspection | Protects quality risk |

### Quality Clauses to Always Insist On
- Written specification agreed before purchase (moisture %, grade, purity)
- Right to test samples at an accredited lab before payment
- Right to reject non-conforming goods (with refund)
- Penalty clause for delivery delay (1–2% per week)
- Replacement obligation for rejected batches within [X days]

## 3. BUYER / SALES NEGOTIATION

### What You're Negotiating
- Selling price (FOB / CIF / DDP)
- Payment method (LC vs TT, advance %)
- Volume commitment (spot vs. long-term)
- Delivery timeline and Incoterms
- Quality claim process and rejection policy
- Packaging and labelling requirements

### Before You Negotiate
- Know the current export benchmark price for your product
- Know your floor price (minimum you can accept and still make margin)
- Know the buyer's alternatives — who else can supply them?
- Know your total cost (from unit economics in DOC 3)

### Pricing Strategy

**For a new buyer (no track record):**
- Start with CIF price (you control freight and insurance)
- Price at current market + 5–8% quality premium
- Justify premium: "Our price reflects Grade [X] quality, pre-shipment
  inspection by [SGS/BV], and [certification name] certification."

**For a repeat buyer:**
- Consider FOB pricing (they manage freight — simpler for you)
- Offer volume discount tiers:
  "For orders above [X tons], we can come down to $[Y]. For [2X tons]
  on a contract, we can offer $[Z] for a 6-month period."

### Payment Terms — What to Push For

| Buyer Risk Level | Push For | Accept If Necessary |
|---|---|---|
| New buyer (unknown) | 100% TT in advance | 50% advance + LC for balance |
| New buyer (verified) | Irrevocable LC | 30% TT + 70% LC |
| Established buyer | 30% advance TT + 70% against BL | Open account (30 days) after 3+ successful shipments |
| Long-term, trusted | Open account (30–60 days) | — |

**Never ship on 100% open account to any buyer in the first year.**

### The Volume-for-Price Framework
"I can offer $[better price] if you can commit to [minimum volume]
per month for [minimum period]. That gives me the certainty to
invest in [better sourcing / processing / certification] and pass
the saving to you."

### Handling the "Your Price is Too High" Objection
1. Don't immediately drop price — ask: "Too high compared to what?"
2. If they name a competitor: "That's interesting — are they offering
   [your quality grade / your certifications / your inspection standard]?
   Our price includes [list what makes you different]."
3. If price must come down, trade for something:
   "I can move on price if you can increase the order to [X] tons,
   or pay 50% in advance, or commit to a 6-month arrangement."

## 4. FREIGHT & LOGISTICS NEGOTIATION

### What You're Negotiating
- Ocean freight rate (per TEU / per ton)
- Air freight rate (per kg / per shipment)
- Transit time and schedule
- Detention and demurrage-free days
- Local charges (THC, documentation fees, fuel surcharges)

### Key Principle
Always get minimum 3 freight forwarder quotes. The spread between
quotes on the same route can be 30–50%. Never accept the first quote.

### Negotiation Framework
"I have a shipment of [X CBM / X tons] from [port] to [destination port].
I'm getting quotes from several forwarders. Your quote is [$X]. I have
a competing quote at [$Y]. Can you match or beat that?"

### Items That Are Usually Negotiable
- Ocean freight rate: ✅ Highly negotiable (market-rate driven)
- Documentation fees: ✅ Often waived for regular clients
- Free days before demurrage: ✅ Negotiate 7–10 free days minimum
- THC (Terminal Handling Charges): ❌ Usually fixed by terminal
- Fuel surcharge (BAF): ❌ Usually fixed

### For Regular Shippers (3+ shipments/year)
"I'm planning [X] shipments per year on this route. If we commit
to working exclusively with you on this lane, what's your best
annual rate?"

Loyalty deals can reduce freight cost 10–20% vs. spot rates.

### Questions to Ask Every Forwarder
- What shipping line do you use on this route? What's their reliability?
- What's the free time at destination port before demurrage kicks in?
- Do you have a track record with [product type] — any special handling needed?
- What are ALL included charges vs. what are extras?
- Do you have a local agent at destination who can assist with customs?

## 5. BANK, LC & TRADE FINANCE NEGOTIATION

### What You're Negotiating
- Interest rate on trade finance / export loan
- LC confirmation charges
- Processing fees and bank charges
- Repayment timeline
- Collateral requirements

### Key Principle
Banks compete for trade finance business. Use multiple bank conversations
simultaneously — not to play games, but to understand the market and
improve your terms.

### Trade Finance Rate Negotiation
"I've spoken with [two other banks] about this facility. The rates
I've been quoted range from [X]% to [Y]%. Given the strength of our
application — [strong cash flow / LC backing / export contract /
government scheme eligibility] — I was hoping to be closer to [X]%."

### Reducing Collateral Requirements
- Ask: "Does this qualify for [government export scheme — NEXIM /
  EXIM bank / development bank]?" — schemes often reduce collateral
- Offer an LC from a strong buyer as collateral instead of property
- Offer personal guarantee for smaller amounts (avoid for large)
- Stock/warehouse receipt: use inventory as collateral for working capital

### LC-Specific Negotiation Points
- Number of document presentation days: push for 21 days (standard is 7)
- Tolerance clause: request +/-5% on quantity and value
- Partial shipment: request permission for partial shipments
- Transhipment: ensure allowed if your route requires it
- Documents: negotiate to remove unnecessary documents that are hard to get

## 6. INVESTOR & GRANT NEGOTIATION

### Equity Investor Negotiation
- Never give away more than 30% equity in a seed round
- Understand the difference between pre-money and post-money valuation
- Key terms to negotiate beyond equity %:
  - Anti-dilution protection (resist this as a founder)
  - Board seat composition (maintain founder majority on board)
  - Information rights (monthly reporting vs. quarterly)
  - Exit preferences (1× liquidation preference is standard; resist 2×)
- Always get a term sheet before any handshake deal
- Always use a lawyer to review investment documents

### Grant Negotiation
Grants are not negotiable on amount — but are negotiable on:
- Reporting frequency (push for quarterly vs. monthly)
- Milestone definition (negotiate achievable milestones)
- Budget line flexibility (push for 10–15% reallocation flexibility)
- Matching fund ratio (sometimes negotiable from 50:50 to 60:40)
- Grant period extension (if milestones need more time)

### Key Phrase for Investors
"We're not looking for just capital — we're looking for a partner
who understands [this market / this product / this region]. Before
we discuss valuation, I'd love to understand what else you bring
to the table beyond the investment."

This shifts you from supplicant to selector — which changes the power dynamic.

## 7. RED LINES — WHAT YOU SHOULD NEVER CONCEDE

These are non-negotiables. Giving these up creates legal, financial,
or reputational risk that can destroy the business.

| Non-Negotiable | Why |
|---|---|
| Shipping without confirmed payment / LC | Buyer default = total loss |
| Skipping pre-shipment inspection | One bad shipment destroys reputation |
| Accepting open account terms from new buyers | Too high counterparty risk |
| Agreeing to supply more than your confirmed stock | Creates breach risk |
| Accepting price changes after goods are shipped | Completely unacceptable |
| Signing supply agreements without quality specs | No recourse if dispute |
| Giving investor control majority without board protection | Lose your company |
| Verbal contracts for orders above $5,000 | No legal standing |

## 8. SCRIPTS & PHRASES THAT WORK

### To Open Price Negotiation
"I want to work with you long-term. To do that, I need the numbers
to make sense. Help me understand — is there any flexibility here?"

### To Respond to "Take it or leave it"
"I respect that. Before I decide, can I ask — what would it take
for this to become a long-term relationship rather than a one-off?
Because that's what I'm looking for."

### To Create Urgency Without Lying
"I have two other suppliers I'm evaluating. I'd prefer to work with
you, but I need to confirm by [date]. Is that workable?"

### To Anchor a Price
"We've been exporting to [region] at $[X]. For a new buyer I'd
normally start at $[higher number], but I know the first shipment
is where trust gets built — so I want to make this work."

### To Close a Deal
"It sounds like we're close. If I [small concession], can we sign
the contract this week?"

### To Walk Away Gracefully
"I appreciate the conversation. At that price/those terms, I can't
make it work right now. If your situation changes, I'd welcome
the chance to revisit it."

## 9. NEGOTIATION PREPARATION CHECKLIST

Before any significant negotiation, complete this:

- [ ] Know your BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement) —
      what you'll do if this deal doesn't happen
- [ ] Know your walk-away price / minimum terms
- [ ] Research 3+ alternatives (other buyers / suppliers / banks)
- [ ] Know the other party's likely constraints and pain points
- [ ] Prepare 3 anchor points (optimistic, realistic, minimum)
- [ ] Decide what you'll trade (volume? timeline? payment?) for what
      you want (better price? better terms?)
- [ ] Know who has decision-making authority on their side
- [ ] Prepare your proof points (certifications, past shipments, references)
- [ ] Decide your opening position (always start better than your target)
- [ ] Prepare your silence (commit to not filling pauses)

# Failure & Exit Protocol — Trade Launch Intelligence
# What to Do When the Business Is Not Working

## Why This Document Exists
Every serious business advisor includes an exit protocol.
Not because they expect failure — but because having a clear plan
if things go wrong reduces panic, protects your assets, preserves
your reputation, and positions you to start again faster.

Reading this document is not pessimistic. It is professional.

## 1. EARLY WARNING SIGNS

These are the signals that the business is in serious trouble.
Address them at the first sign — not after three months.

### Financial Warning Signs
- Cash runway drops below 6 weeks
- Gross margin falls below break-even for 2+ consecutive months
- A key buyer cancels or significantly reduces orders
- A major supplier defaults leaving you unable to fulfil commitments
- Revenue is consistently below 60% of plan for 3+ months
- You are borrowing to pay operating expenses (salary, rent, utilities)
- Outstanding receivables are growing and not being collected

### Operational Warning Signs
- Product quality failures on 2+ consecutive shipments
- A key certification is revoked or rejected
- Your main sourcing region faces severe disruption (ban, conflict, crop failure)
- You are unable to get goods to port within agreed timelines
- Your key supplier relationship breaks down irreparably

### Market Warning Signs
- Commodity price collapses more than 30% below your cost base
- Your target country introduces an import ban on your product
- A major competitor undercuts you at a price you cannot match
- Demand in your primary market drops sharply with no recovery signal

## 2. TRIAGE PROTOCOL — STOP THE BLEEDING

When you identify a crisis, do these 5 things immediately — in this order:

### Step 1 — Get a Clear Financial Picture (Day 1)
- Write down exactly how much cash you have right now
- List every amount owed TO you (receivables) — with likelihood of collection
- List every amount you owe (payables) — with due dates
- Calculate your true runway: cash + collectible receivables − unavoidable payables
- You cannot make decisions without knowing this number exactly

### Step 2 — Pause All Non-Essential Spending (Day 1)
- Stop all discretionary spending immediately
- Defer any payments that can legally be deferred
- Cancel or pause any subscriptions, memberships, paid listings
- Do not take on new inventory unless it is pre-sold
- Do not hire, even if you were planning to

### Step 3 — Communicate Proactively (Days 2–3)
- Contact any buyers expecting shipments — be honest about timeline
- Contact suppliers you owe money to — most prefer a restructured payment to a default
- Contact your bank BEFORE you miss a payment — banks help borrowers who communicate early
- Do NOT go silent with anyone you have financial obligations to
  (Silence is the most damaging thing you can do to relationships and credit)

### Step 4 — Protect Revenue (Days 3–5)
- Chase all outstanding receivables immediately
- Offer a small discount for early payment if needed (2–3% for 7-day payment)
- Explore invoice discounting — sell confirmed invoices to a factoring company for immediate cash
- Check if any grants you applied for can be accelerated

### Step 5 — Make the Hard Decision (Week 2)
Once you have a clear picture, choose one of three paths:
- **Rescue** — the business can be saved with specific interventions (see Section 3)
- **Wind-down** — the business cannot be saved but can be closed cleanly (see Section 4)
- **Pause** — the business is viable but needs time to recover (structured break)

## 3. RESCUE OPTIONS — BEFORE YOU EXIT

Explore these before deciding to close:

### Option A — Pivot the Product
If your product is the problem (price collapse, import ban, demand shift):
- Can you sell the same product to a different market?
- Can you process it differently to access a higher-value segment?
- Can you blend it with another product to create a new offering?
- Can you sell your existing stock at a lower price to recover cash?

### Option B — Pivot the Market
If your target market is the problem:
- Run Phase 5 of the skill again — are there better destination markets?
- Could you sell domestically what you planned to export?
- Is there a regional market (neighbouring countries) that's more accessible?

### Option C — Restructure Finances
- Renegotiate loan repayment terms with your bank
- Approach development finance institutions (NEXIM, BOI, regional DFIs) for emergency refinancing
- Bring in a partner who contributes capital in exchange for equity
- Sell a % of the business to raise cash without full closure
- Apply for emergency grants (some government programs exist for distressed SMEs)

### Option D — Strategic Partnership / Acquisition
- Could a larger player in your industry acquire your business or assets?
- Could a competitor or complementary business merge with you?
- Could you become a supplier to a larger exporter rather than exporting yourself?

### Option E — Managed Contraction
- Scale back to your profitable core — if one product/market works, focus only there
- Release staff, reduce rent, cut to minimum viable operations
- Stabilise at smaller scale, then rebuild

## 4. GRACEFUL WIND-DOWN PROCESS

If rescue is not viable, close cleanly. How you close defines whether
you can start again.

### Step 1 — Stop Taking New Orders and Commitments
Immediately. Do not take advance payments you cannot fulfil.
This is both legally and ethically critical.

### Step 2 — Fulfil All Existing Commitments Where Possible
- Deliver any paid orders where goods are available
- If you cannot fulfil: contact the buyer immediately, explain, and arrange refund
- Never keep advance payments for goods you cannot deliver

### Step 3 — Collect All Outstanding Receivables
- Send formal demand letters to all outstanding debtors
- For significant amounts: engage a collections lawyer or debt recovery firm
- Consider settling for 70–80 cents on the dollar for faster collection
- Write off genuinely uncollectable amounts — do not spend more collecting than the amount

### Step 4 — Settle All Payables
Priority order:
1. Staff wages (legal priority in most countries — pay first)
2. Tax obligations (significant penalties for non-payment)
3. Secured loans (bank / development finance — they hold collateral)
4. Suppliers (negotiate partial payment if necessary)
5. Unsecured creditors
6. Other obligations

### Step 5 — Liquidate Assets
- Sell remaining inventory — even at below cost to recover cash
- Sell equipment, vehicles, fixtures — local auctions or online platforms
- Sub-lease warehouse if remaining on a fixed term
- Cancel all subscriptions, registrations, and ongoing costs

### Step 6 — Close Formally
- File for formal dissolution / deregistration with the company registry
- Deregister for VAT / tax (important — ongoing filing obligations otherwise)
- Close business bank accounts after all transactions clear
- Cancel export agency registration and any product certifications
- Keep records for the statutory period (typically 5–7 years by law)

### Step 7 — Notify All Stakeholders
- Write a brief, professional notification to key buyers, suppliers, partners
- Thank them for the relationship — do not burn bridges
- If you plan to start again: say so
- No drama, no blame, no lengthy explanations — brief and professional

## 5. PROTECTING YOUR PERSONAL ASSETS

### If You Have a Registered Company (Limited Liability)
- A limited liability company legally separates your personal assets from business debts
- Creditors can pursue company assets — not your personal home, car, or savings
- EXCEPTION: If you personally guaranteed any loans — you are personally liable for those
- EXCEPTION: If courts find "piercing of the corporate veil" (fraud, mixing personal/company funds)

### If You Are a Sole Trader / Unregistered Business
- You are personally liable for all business debts
- Creditors can pursue your personal assets
- Action: If debts are significant, consult a lawyer immediately

### What to Do Now (Before Any Crisis)
- Ensure all business transactions go through the business bank account — NEVER mix personal and business money
- Review all loans — are any personally guaranteed? Know your exposure
- Check your personal savings are in your personal name only — separate from business
- Understand the insolvency law in your country — protections vary significantly

## 6. HANDLING OUTSTANDING CONTRACTS & OBLIGATIONS

### Unfulfilled Buyer Orders
- Notify the buyer immediately in writing
- Return advance payments as quickly as possible
- If goods are partially available: offer partial fulfillment with partial refund
- Do not offer empty promises of future delivery — be honest

### Outstanding Supplier Payments
- Contact suppliers before they contact you
- Propose a restructured payment plan — most prefer this to total loss
- "I can pay [X]% now and the balance over [Y] months" — most suppliers will accept
- Get any agreed restructuring in writing

### Export Documentation in Progress
- Notify the export promotion agency if you are withdrawing
- Cancel any freight bookings (get refund policies in writing — act fast)
- Inform customs of any pending declarations

### Loans in Default or at Risk
- Contact your bank BEFORE missing a payment — always
- Request a repayment holiday (many banks offer 3–6 month moratoriums)
- Explore: Is the loan covered by export credit insurance? This may protect the bank and you
- If the loan has a government guarantee (NEXIM, BOI etc.): contact them directly

## 7. RECOVERING CAPITAL WHERE POSSIBLE

### From Buyers Who Owe You
- Send formal demand letter (first class / recorded delivery)
- Follow with legal letter from lawyer (often unlocks payment without going to court)
- Small Claims Court / Commercial Court for significant amounts
- Engage a debt collection agency (they typically take 15–30% of amount recovered)

### From Your Business Assets
- Inventory: sell at market, below market, or to brokers
- Equipment: online marketplaces, local auctions, competitors who might buy
- Intellectual property / certifications: may have value to a buyer of your business
- Customer relationships: may be saleable to a competitor

### From Insurance
- Review all policies for business interruption coverage
- Review marine/cargo insurance for any unfulfilled shipments
- Review trade credit insurance if you had it — buyer defaults may be claimable
- Act quickly — insurance claims have strict notification deadlines

## 8. PROTECTING YOUR REPUTATION & RELATIONSHIPS

Your reputation in trade is your most valuable long-term asset.
Even in failure, how you behave is remembered.

### What Earns Long-Term Respect
- Communicating proactively before problems become public
- Paying what you committed to pay, in the order that's fair
- Fulfilling what you can fulfil, and being honest about what you cannot
- Thanking suppliers, buyers, and partners for the relationship
- Taking personal responsibility without blaming others

### What Destroys Future Opportunities
- Going silent with creditors
- Keeping advance payments you cannot refund
- Blaming others publicly
- Making promises you know you cannot keep to buy time
- Disappearing from the market without explanation

### The 72-Hour Rule
If something goes wrong that affects a counterparty, notify them within 72 hours.
This single rule preserves more relationships than any other action.

## 10. STARTING AGAIN — THE RESET PLAYBOOK

Most successful traders have at least one failure behind them.
The difference between those who succeed eventually and those who don't
is that the successful ones extracted the lessons, protected their
reputation, and started the next attempt smarter.

### The Reset Framework

**Wait before restarting** — Give yourself 30–90 days. Process what happened.
Rushing into the next venture before extracting lessons repeats mistakes.

**Choose a lower-risk starting point** — If you lost on exports, try local
first. If you over-leveraged, start smaller. If you had one buyer, start
with three. Apply the lessons directly.

**Rebuild credibility first** — Before raising money or making commitments,
rebuild 3–5 positive relationships. Small, successful transactions rebuild
trust faster than any explanation.

**Use this skill again** — Run the full skill on your next product from scratch.
But this time, your lessons document feeds directly into a more accurate
risk assessment (DOC 6) and more realistic financial projections (DOC 3).

**Tell your story honestly** — Investors and partners who have failed themselves
(most good ones have) respect honest reflection far more than pretending
it didn't happen. "I tried X, here's what I learned, here's why Y is different"
is a compelling narrative.

**The question to answer before starting again:**
"What specifically is different this time that makes success more likely?"
If you cannot answer this in 3 concrete sentences — wait longer.

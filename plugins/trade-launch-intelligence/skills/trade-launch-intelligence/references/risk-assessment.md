## 1. Risk Scoring Methodology

### Scoring Scale
**Likelihood (L):** How often is this risk likely to occur?
- 1 = Rare (< 5% chance)
- 2 = Unlikely (5–20%)
- 3 = Possible (20–50%)
- 4 = Likely (50–80%)
- 5 = Almost Certain (> 80%)

**Impact (I):** If it occurs, how badly does it affect the business?
- 1 = Negligible (< 5% revenue impact)
- 2 = Minor (5–15% revenue impact)
- 3 = Moderate (15–35% revenue impact)
- 4 = Major (35–65% revenue impact)
- 5 = Catastrophic (> 65% or business-ending)

**Risk Score = L × I**
- 1–5: 🟢 Low Risk
- 6–12: 🟡 Medium Risk
- 15–25: 🔴 High Risk

## 3. Nigeria-Specific Risk Factors

These risks are particularly acute for businesses operating from Nigeria:

### A. Infrastructure Risk
- **Power outages**: Near-certain; budget ₦200K–₦800K/month for generator
- **Road infrastructure**: Factor extra transport time + cost, especially rainy season
- **Internet reliability**: Affects B2B communication; use backup data/broadband
- **Cold chain gaps**: Critical for perishables; limited cold storage infrastructure outside Lagos

### B. Policy & Regulatory Risk
- **Sudden export bans**: Nigeria has historically banned export of maize, other crops during shortage; monitor FMARD policy
- **FX restrictions**: CBN has imposed forex controls before; structure payments to minimize trapped FX
- **Port policy changes**: NCS/NIMASA policies can change handling costs significantly
- **Import duty changes**: If importing equipment/packaging, tariffs can change annually (Finance Act)

### C. Political & Security Risk
- **Banditry/kidnapping in North**: Affects sourcing trips to sesame, groundnut, shea regions
- **Port disruptions**: Can affect main export port — research your country's port history
- **Election cycles**: Business environment sometimes disrupted around elections
- **Insecurity in NE Nigeria**: Limit sourcing exposure in conflict-affected LGAs

### D. Counterparty Risk
- **Advance payment fraud**: Common in agro commodity deals; never pay 100% upfront
- **Documentation fraud**: Falsified weight/quality certificates — use accredited inspectors
- **Ghost suppliers**: Always visit physically before first order
- **Fake buyers**: International buyers who vanish after receiving goods — always use LC

## 4. Export Market Risk by Region

### European Union (EU)
- **Regulatory burden**: High — SPS, RASFF, MRL compliance for food
- **Payment risk**: Low — established banking systems, LC readily available
- **Market access risk**: Low — stable, long-term demand
- **Key risk**: Phytosanitary non-compliance → shipment rejection/destruction (very costly)
- **Mitigation**: EU-accredited testing labs; GlobalGAP certification helps

### United States (USA)
- **Regulatory burden**: High — FDA, USDA, FSMA, CBP
- **Payment risk**: Low — TT/LC available
- **Trade tariff risk**: Medium — US trade policy can shift; check AGOA annually
- **Key risk**: FDA red listing of Nigeria (some products historically red-flagged)
- **Mitigation**: FDA facility registration; FSMA compliance; US-based import broker

### Middle East / UAE
- **Regulatory burden**: Medium
- **Payment risk**: Medium — UAE is reliable; other GCC varies
- **Halal requirement**: High — mandatory for food/cosmetics in most GCC states
- **Key risk**: Payment default from smaller traders; use Dubai Multi Commodities Centre (DMCC) verified buyers
- **Mitigation**: Halal certification; trade via DMCC-registered counterparties

### China
- **Regulatory burden**: High — GACC registration mandatory for food exporters
- **Payment risk**: Medium-High — be cautious; use LC from reputable Chinese banks (ICBC, Bank of China)
- **Price negotiation risk**: Chinese buyers are very price-sensitive; margins can compress
- **Key risk**: Payment disputes, quality disputes, IP theft (for value-added products)
- **Mitigation**: GACC registration; use experienced China trade lawyers; never ship without LC

### West Africa (ECOWAS)
- **Regulatory burden**: Low-Medium — ECOWAS TLP reduces barriers
- **Payment risk**: Medium-High — use advance TT; LC not always available in smaller markets
- **Infrastructure risk**: High — border crossings, port infrastructure challenges
- **Key risk**: Border closure, product seizure, informal levies
- **Mitigation**: Local agent/distributor in each country; understand informal trade dynamics

### India
- **Regulatory burden**: Medium
- **Payment risk**: Medium — Indian buyers sometimes slow payers; use LC
- **Price sensitivity**: Very high — Indian buyers negotiate aggressively
- **Key risk**: Quality rejection disputes; long payment cycles
- **Mitigation**: Use LC; target tier-1 importers (listed on APEDA database)

## 5. Risk Mitigation Playbook

### Financial Risk Playbook
```
ACTION 1: Open a USD domiciliary account (first week)
ACTION 2: Never accept more than 30% exposure to one buyer
ACTION 3: Price all export contracts in USD/EUR, collect in forex
ACTION 4: Maintain 3-month expense reserve in naira at all times
ACTION 5: Get marine cargo insurance for every shipment (non-negotiable)
ACTION 6: Use Irrevocable LC for new buyers; TT (30% advance + 70% BL) for trusted
ACTION 7: Subscribe to commodity price alerts (AFEX, Reuters, Tridge)
```

### Operational Risk Playbook
```
ACTION 1: Use only BOFFA-registered freight forwarders
ACTION 2: Require pre-shipment inspection certificate from SGS/Bureau Veritas
ACTION 3: Maintain 3 approved suppliers per product; don't depend on one
ACTION 4: Visit every supplier at least once before large orders
ACTION 5: Build 2-week buffer into all export timelines (for port delays)
ACTION 6: Test product quality in accredited lab before every export consignment
ACTION 7: Get fumigation certificate for ALL agro products (even if not required by buyer)
```

### Regulatory Risk Playbook
```
ACTION 1: Subscribe to your country's export agency and customs authority policy updates
ACTION 2: Hire a licensed customs agent (licensed by NCS) for all exports
ACTION 3: Start all product certification applications 6+ months before planned first export
ACTION 4: Get Certificate of Origin for EVERY shipment (enables tariff preferences)
ACTION 5: Monitor destination country's import ban lists quarterly
ACTION 6: Maintain a lawyer on retainer who specialises in trade law
```

## 6. Contingency Planning

### If Your Shipment is Rejected at Destination
1. Contact your freight forwarder immediately
2. Determine reason: quality, documentation, banned substance, wrong labeling
3. If quality: negotiate with buyer for price reduction (partial acceptance)
4. If documentation: rush correct documents via DHL to destination customs
5. If abandoned: file marine insurance claim
6. Lesson: Always conduct pre-shipment inspection and lab testing

### If Your Main Supplier Fails Mid-Contract
1. Activate secondary supplier immediately
2. Negotiate delivery timeline extension with buyer (most understand)
3. Source emergency stock at premium from broker/market (budget for this)
4. Inform buyer proactively — silence destroys trust, transparency saves it
5. Future: sign supply contracts with penalty clauses

### If the FX Rate Spikes Against You
1. Calculate actual margin impact
2. If contract allows, renegotiate price with buyer
3. If not possible: absorb loss on this shipment, learn to hedge next time
4. Hedge options: domiciliary account (collect and hold forex), forward rate agreement with bank

### If a Key License is Delayed
1. Work with a licensed local regulatory consultant — they typically cut certification time by 30–50%
2. Start applications simultaneously, not sequentially
3. Pay for express processing where the authority offers it
4. Never promise buyers a shipment date before all licenses are confirmed
